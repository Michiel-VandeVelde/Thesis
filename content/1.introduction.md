## Introduction

{:#introduction}
Streaming has become a central part of daily digital life. In 2024,
statistics from the Flemish government indicate that 41% of residents
in the Flemish Region watch series and 40% listen to music at least
once per week. This widespread streaming activity reflects a broader
trend of growing digital engagement: 94% of residents aged 18 and
older use the internet at least once per week, with 96% reporting
weekly use of a smartphone or mobile phone and 83% using a computer,
laptop, or tablet on a weekly basis [1].

As digital engagement continues to grow, so does the volume of data
generated by users. This growth is accompanied by rising concerns
about how such data is managed. Studies from SolidLab an imec
research initiative investigating user-centric personal data
management indicate that both trust and perceptions of transparent
data usage remain critically low. For instance, survey results show that a
significant portion of the Flemish population believes that companies
are insufficiently transparent about how personal data is used, while
approximately 70% of users report a lack of control over their own
data [2].

This tension is further deepened by the structure of today's data
economy. It is often argued that “data is the new gold.” However, this
analogy is fundamentally flawed. Unlike gold, data can be shared
simultaneously with multiple parties without being depleted a
property that makes concentration of data control arguably wasteful
from a societal perspective.
Despite this, data flows predominantly toward a relatively small
number of organizations, particularly large tech companies based in
the United States and China. These companies manage data within their
own infrastructure and derive significant economic value from it. This
concentration has implications not only for privacy, but also for 
**data sovereignty and national security**, while simultaneously acting
as a barrier to innovation: smaller and emerging companies are
frequently excluded from the data ecosystems they need in order to compete on
equal footing [3].

The European Union has recognized data concentration as a structural
problem, responding with substantial investment through initiatives
such as the Digital Europe Programme [4] and GAIA-X [5]. These
programs envision a human-centric data economy in which individuals
retain meaningful control over their personal data. The gap
between policy ambition and practical implementation remains
significant, particularly in the media domain, where behavioral data is
continuously generated but governance frameworks for user-centric
control are still immature.

Two promising building blocks for such an architecture are the Solid
ecosystem and TrustFlows. Solid enables users to store their personal
data in decentralized online data stores called pods that they manage
and control, allowing them to grant or revoke access to applications
as needed. TrustFlows complements this by providing a framework for
building trustworthy data flows that are interoperable, legally
compliant, and user-centric. Together, they provide a foundation for
rethinking how personal media data is stored, shared, and governed.

This bachelor thesis, conducted at imec-IDLab, addresses the following
research question:
Which key design decisions need to be made when building media
behavior profiles across multiple media services?

To answer this question, competency questions are first formulated to
define the functional requirements of the proposed architecture and
validated with stakeholders from the media sector, including VRT and
MediaNet Vlaanderen. This is complemented by a literature review of
relevant technologies and open standards. The findings culminate in
both a designed architecture and a working prototype, demonstrating
that user-centric management of personal media data is not only
theoretically sound but also practically achievable.

The outcome of this research is a conceptual architecture, a
proof-of-concept implementation, and a structured set of design
decisions for cross-service media behavior profiling.

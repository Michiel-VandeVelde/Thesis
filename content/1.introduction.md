
## Introduction

{:#introduction}
In 2024, statistics from the Flemish government indicate that 94% of residents in the Flemish Region aged 18 and older used the internet at least once per week. Additionally, 96% reported weekly use of a smartphone or mobile phone, while 83% used a computer, laptop, or tablet on a weekly basis. Streaming activities are also widespread, with 41% of the population watching series and 40% listening to music at least once per week [1].

As digital engagement continues to grow, so does the volume of data generated by users. However, this increase is accompanied by rising concerns about how such data is managed. Studies from the SolidLab project reveal that trust in and perceptions of transparent data usage remain critically low. A significant portion of the Flemish population believes that companies are insufficiently transparent about how personal data is used, and approximately seven out of ten users report a lack of control over their own data [2].

This tension is further deepened by the structure of today's data economy. It is often argued that "data is the new gold." However, this analogy is fundamentally flawed. Unlike gold, data can be shared simultaneously with multiple parties without being depleted. Hoarding data is therefore inefficient and socially undesirable. Despite this, data flows predominantly toward a relatively small number of organizations, particularly large tech companies based in the United States and China. These companies manage data within their own infrastructure and derive significant economic value from it. This concentration not only has profound implications for privacy, but also acts as a barrier to innovation: smaller and emerging companies are frequently excluded from the data ecosystems they need to compete on equal footing [3].

In response, the European Union is investing billions of euros in data initiatives aimed at strengthening control, interoperability, and trust across data ecosystems. The European Commission's European Strategy for Data envisions a single market in which data can flow freely across sectors and borders, while remaining secure and aligned with European values such as privacy, transparency, and user control. Central to this vision is a human-centric approach, in which individuals retain meaningful control over their personal data.

Yet translating these policy ambitions into working, user-centric solutions remains a significant challenge, particularly in the media domain, where large-scale behavioral data such as viewing and listening histories are continuously generated and almost exclusively controlled by centralized platforms. What is needed is a concrete architecture that restores control to the user without sacrificing interoperability or usability.

Two promising building blocks for such an architecture are the Solid ecosystem and Trustflows. Solid enables users to store their personal data in decentralized online data stores called pods that they own and control, allowing them to grant or revoke access to applications as needed. Trustflows complements this by providing a framework for building trustworthy data flows that are interoperable, legally compliant, and user-centric. Together, they offer a foundation for rethinking how personal media data is stored, shared, and governed.

This bachelor thesis, conducted at imec-IDLab, addresses the following research questions: How can an architecture be designed for managing viewing and listening histories in a user-centric manner, leveraging the Solid and TrustFlows ecosystems alongside relevant open standards? And how can this architecture be validated through a concrete technical implementation?

To answer these questions, competency questions are first formulated to define the functional requirements of the proposed architecture and validated with stakeholders from the media sector, including VRT and MediaNet Vlaanderen. This is complemented by a literature review of relevant technologies and open standards. The findings culminate in both a designed architecture and a working prototype, together demonstrating that user-centric management of personal media data is not only theoretically sound but practically achievable.

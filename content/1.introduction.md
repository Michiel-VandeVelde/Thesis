
## Introduction

{:#introduction}

In 2024, statistics from the Flemish government indicate that 94% of residents in the Flemish Region aged 18 and older use the internet at least once per week. Additionally, 96% report weekly use of a smartphone or mobile phone, while 83% use a computer, laptop, or tablet on a weekly basis. Streaming activities are also widespread, with 41% of the population watching series and 40% listening to music at least once per week [Mediagebruik](cite:cites vlaanderen-mediagebruik-2024).

As digital engagement continues to grow, so does the volume of data generated by users. However, this increase is accompanied by rising concerns about how such data is managed. Studies from the SolidLab project indicate that trust in and perceptions of transparent data usage remain critically low. For instance, survey results show that a significant portion of the Flemish population believes that companies are insufficiently transparent about how personal data is used, while approximately 70% of users report a lack of control over their own data [SOLID Monitor 2024](cite:cites solid-monitor-2024).

This tension is further deepened by the structure of today's data economy. It is often argued that “data is the new gold.” However, this analogy is fundamentally flawed. Unlike gold, data can be shared simultaneously with multiple parties without being depleted, making exclusive control over data economically and socially undesirable [Data mogen niet het nieuwe goud zijn](cite:cites imec-data-niet-goud-2022). Despite this, data flows predominantly toward a relatively small number of organizations, particularly large tech companies based in the United States and China. These companies manage data within their own infrastructure and derive significant economic value from it. This concentration has implications not only for privacy, but also for **data sovereignty and national security**, while simultaneously acting as a barrier to innovation: smaller and emerging companies are frequently excluded from the data ecosystems they need to compete on equal footing [Data mogen niet het nieuwe goud zijn](cite:cites imec-data-niet-goud-2022).

The European Union has recognized data concentration as a structural problem, responding with substantial investment through initiatives such as the [Digital Europe Programme](cite:cites digital-europe-programme) and [GAIA-X](cite:cites gaia-x). These programs envision a human-centric data economy in which individuals retain meaningful control over their personal data. However, the gap between policy ambition and practical implementation remains significant particularly in the media domain, where behavioral data is continuously generated but governance frameworks for user-centric control are still immature.

Two promising building blocks for such an architecture are the Solid ecosystem and TrustFlows. Solid enables users to store their personal data in decentralized online data stores called pods that they manage and control, allowing them to grant or revoke access to applications as needed. TrustFlows complements this by providing a framework for building trustworthy data flows that are interoperable, legally compliant, and user-centric. Together, they provide a foundation for rethinking how personal media data is stored, shared, and governed.

This bachelor thesis, conducted at imec-IDLab, addresses the following research question:
Which key design decisions need to be made when building media behavior profiles across multiple media services?

To answer this question, competency questions are first formulated to define the functional requirements of the proposed architecture and validated with stakeholders from the media sector, including VRT and MediaNet Vlaanderen. This is complemented by a literature review of relevant technologies and open standards. The findings culminate in both a designed architecture and a working prototype, demonstrating that user-centric management of personal media data is not only theoretically sound but also practically achievable.

The outcome of this research is a conceptual architecture, a proof-of-concept implementation, and a structured set of design decisions for cross-service media behavior profiling.

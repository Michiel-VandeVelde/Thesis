## Performance analysis
{:#perf}


While the primary focus of Traqula is flexibility and expressivity,
sufficient performance is still important in broadly used software tools.
As such, we evaluate the execution-time performance of Traqula, comparing its different parsing modes,
contrasting it with [SPARQL.js](cite:cites sparqljs) plus [SPARQLAlgebra.js](cite:cites sparqlalgebrajs), and highlighting the impact of parser reuse.<span class="comment" data-author="RT">You should probably explain how SPARQL.js and SPARQLAlgebra.js are related to each other.</span>
All measurements were conducted on Fedora Linux 43 (Workstation Edition), equipped with an Intel® Core™ Ultra 7 165U × 14 processor, having 16 GiB of RAM.
We measured the execution times of parsing 199 SPARQL 1.1 queries <span class="comment" data-author="RT">Where do the queries come from? How can you argue that they are representative of the real world?</span> in each of the tools.

[](#traqula-bench) shows the execution times of Traqula across different targets and parser configurations.
Patching a modular SPARQL 1.1 parser to support SPARQL 1.2 results in a small but statistically significant increase in execution time (p < 0.05),
with a mean increase of 6.13% <!-- 5.71/5.38 -->.
Tracking source information to support round-tripping introduces additional overhead due to the more complex lexing process,
with mean increases of  16.5% <!-- 6.27/5.38 -->
and 17.5% <!-- 6.71/5.71 --> for SPARQL 1.1 and 1.2 parsing respectively (both p < 0.05).
Parsing directly into algebra, effectively performing an AST transformation after parsing, further increases execution time,
with mean increases of 58.7% <!-- 8.54/5.38 --> for SPARQL 1.1 and 63.4% <!-- 9.33/5.71 --> for SPARQL 1.2 (p < 0.05).

Despite these relative increases, [](#traqula-sparqljs) shows Traqula remains substantially faster than existing solutions.
The mean execution time for parsing into an AST using Traqula is 5.38 ms, compared to 38.17 ms for SPARQL.js, a 709.5% difference,
even though Traqula generates a more complete and correct AST.
Parsing into algebra shows a similar trend: Traqula takes 8.54 ms, while SPARQLAlgebra.js (using SPARQL.js) requires 39.69 ms, a 464.8% difference.
Much of this advantage can be attributed to the performance of Chevrotain<span class="comment" data-author="RT">I just realized you never actually explained that Traqula is built on Chevrotain. This should be elaborated on somewhere earlier.</span>, the parser toolkit underlying Traqula.

Finally, it is important to note the cost associated with parser creation in a non-pre-compiled language like TypeScript.
Traqula optimizes the parser at runtime<span class="comment" data-author="RT">Not exactly, it's the JavaScript runtime (in this case V8) that uses optimizing compilers. Important to be accurate about this. Maybe even cite a V8 blog post about optimizing compilers.</span>, making parser construction computationally expensive.
To illustrate, parsing the 199 queries with a prebuilt parser has a mean execution time of 5.38 ms,
whereas creating a new parser for each query results in a mean execution time of 2,572.03 ms; a slowdown of 478 times.
This underscores the importance of reusing parser instances wherever possible, which is what is currently done within the Comunica query engine for example.

## Literature Study

The central research question of this thesis asks which design decisions are required when building media behaviour profiles across multiple services in a user-centric way. Answering this question requires a clear understanding of what such profiles are, why current approaches fall short, and which technologies and architectural principles can support a more user-centric solution.


### Media Behaviour Profiles: Definition and Privacy Implications

Media behaviour data, as used in this thesis, refers to all data generated when a user interacts with a media platform. This includes low-level actions such as playing or pausing a video, skipping a track, or submitting a search query, as well as higher-level patterns derived from these actions over time, such as preferred genres, typical usage hours, or cross-platform content preferences.

Individually, these data points may seem insignificant, but when combined they form detailed behavioural profiles that can reveal sensitive characteristics such as cultural identity, political orientation, mental state, or daily routines. This issue is compounded by the fact that most platforms store this data centrally, with little transparency about what is retained, for how long, or who it is shared with.

This lack of transparency creates a clear imbalance: platforms accumulate extensive knowledge about users, while users have very limited insight into, or control over, the profiles built from their behaviour. Addressing this imbalance is a central motivation for the architectural approach explored in this thesis.


### Towards a User-Centric Architecture: Core Principles

A user-centric media profile architecture inverts the traditional platform-controlled model. Instead of each platform storing and governing user data independently, the user maintains their data in a personal data store. Platforms may only access or contribute to this data with explicit user consent, for a clearly defined purpose, and within a specified time frame. This shift gives rise to several core requirements that together shape the design decisions examined throughout this thesis.

**Sovereignty** is a foundational principle in the European data landscape, ensuring that individuals or organisations retain full control over the data they generate. Without sovereignty as an explicit design constraint, any user-centric system risks devolving into another form of delegated control, where users technically have access too their data but lack the practical means to govern it.

**Interoperability and Open Standards** are equally critical. Just as the internet depends on shared protocols such as HTTP, HTML, and URLs, user-centric systems require common standards to enable seamless communication between autonomous services. This need is well illustrated by Conway's Law, which observes that system design tends to mirror the communication structures of the organisations that build it. Without deliberate interoperability, data silos will inevitably persist, not because of malicious intent, but as a natural consequence of organisational boundaries.

**Compliance** in decentralised ecosystems cannot rely on a single governing authority to enforce the rules. Instead, it must be achieved through standardised rules, verification frameworks, and alignment with regulatory requirements such as the GDPR. Initiatives such as Gaia-X introduce mechanisms like Digital Clearing Houses to ensure that data exchanges remain trusted and auditable across organisational boundaries.

**Auditability** follows directly from the compliance requirement: all data access and usage must be traceable and verifiable. Without transparent records of who accessed data, when, and for what purpose, accountability becomes impossible and user trust cannot be sustained.

### The Solid Ecosystem as a Technical Foundation

> "When I invented the World Wide Web, I envisioned technology that would empower people and enable collaboration… Solid returns the web to its roots by giving everyone direct control over their own data."
> - Sir Tim Berners-Lee

The Solid initiative provides a concrete technological foundation for user-centric data management. Its central concept is the **data pod** (or data vault), a decentralised storage space where individuals manage their own information independently of any platform. Users determine who can access their data, for what purpose, and for how long, directly supporting the principle of data sovereignty by shifting control away from centralised intermediaries.

Two access control mechanisms are defined within Solid. **Web Access Control (WAC)** is a widely implemented model, conceptually similar to file system permissions, where resources, users, and groups are identified using URIs. **Access Control Policies (ACP)** offer a more flexible and expressive alternative, enabling more complex, context-sensitive rule definitions. Underpinning both is the separation of **authorisation from storage**: the entity hosting a pod cannot override the access rules defined by the user. This separation is what makes Solid meaningfully sovereign rather than merely decentralised in appearance.

Authentication is handled through **Solid-OIDC**, which builds on the widely adopted OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect standards. Users are identified through a **WebID**, a globally unique, user-controlled URI, enabling identity verification across services without exposing cross-service usage patterns. For deployment within the European context, the **Community Solid Server (CSS)**, developed by imec and Ghent University, offers a production-ready, open-source implementation aligned with European infrastructure requirements.

While Solid provides a strong foundation for storage and access control, it does not by itself address broader concerns such as policy enforcement, cross-service data aggregation, or legal compliance. These require additional frameworks, which are discussed in the sections that follow.

### Best Practices

#### Data Storage and the Solid Pod

A Solid pod serves as a decentralised storage environment where users retain full control over their personal data. By limiting what platforms can access to only what is explicitly permitted, this approach directly supports data minimisation, preventing services from collecting information unrelated to the purpose for which access was granted. Hosting pods on European infrastructure further ensures that data processing aligns with frameworks such as the GDPR, avoiding the jurisdictional risks associated with storage on non-European cloud providers.

In addition to CSS, newer systems such as **Kvasir** are emerging as complementary implementations. Kvasir is a cloud-native, microservice-based platform designed for scalable, data-intensive Solid applications, offering enhanced performance capabilities for deployments that go beyond what CSS is optimised to handle.

[https://kvasir.pages.ilabt.imec.be/kvasir-server/what-is-kvasir.html#usage-scenarios](https://kvasir.pages.ilabt.imec.be/kvasir-server/what-is-kvasir.html#usage-scenarios)

#### Identity and Authentication

A unified identity mechanism is essential for linking data across services while preserving privacy. In this architecture, that role is fulfilled by **WebID**, which provides each user with a globally unique, self-controlled identifier that can be used across any Solid-compatible service. Authentication is performed using **Solid-OIDC**, which allows services to verify a user's identity without gaining visibility into that user's interactions with other services. Combined with the separation of authorisation from storage, this ensures that users retain full control over their access policies, enforced through either WAC or ACP, regardless of where their pod is hosted.

#### Policy Layer and TrustFlows

Storing data in a user-controlled pod is necessary but not sufficient. Without a principled mechanism for governing how that data may be used, access control rules alone cannot guarantee that platforms respect the intent behind a user's consent. Data exchanges are therefore governed by policies expressed in **ODRL** (Open Digital Rights Language), a W3C standard that can encode the purpose of data access, the retention period, conditions for further sharing, and mechanisms for withdrawing consent. A policy engine verifies compliance before any data is accessed, ensuring that every interaction aligns with both user consent and applicable regulatory requirements. This moves governance from a reactive to a proactive posture: rather than auditing violations after the fact, compliance is enforced at the point of access.

#### Aggregation and Cross-Service Profiles

Constructing a cross-service media behaviour profile requires combining data from multiple platforms, each of which may store data in different formats and under different access conditions. This thesis proposes a **user-authorised automated agent** that periodically aggregates permitted data across services. The agent reads authorised data from the user's pod, computes aggregated insights, and stores the results in a designated location within that same pod. The user retains full control throughout this process and can delete the generated profile at any time.

To improve efficiency in environments where data is continuously generated, event streaming techniques such as **LDES** (Linked Data Event Streams) can be employed. Rather than reprocessing entire datasets on each aggregation cycle, LDES enables the agent to consume only incremental updates, significantly reducing computational overhead and latency as the dataset grows over time.

#### Trust and Auditability

Trust in a decentralised system cannot be assumed; it must be actively constructed and maintained. In the proposed architecture, this is achieved through several complementary mechanisms. All participants are verified through their WebID, ensuring that no anonymous or unverifiable actor can access user data. Audit logs are stored directly within the user's pod, providing transparent and tamper-evident records of who accessed data, when, and for what purpose. These records are accessible to the user and can serve as evidence in the event of a compliance dispute. Together, these mechanisms ensure that the architecture aligns with European regulations such as the GDPR and the Data Governance Act, embedding accountability and transparency by design rather than as an afterthought.


### Dataspace Integration (Future Work)

While the architecture described in this thesis focuses on individual data sovereignty, it is designed to be extensible toward broader European data space initiatives. Unlike individual data control mechanisms, data spaces enable **federated data sharing across organisations**, a model in which personal data remains within user-controlled pods, only aggregated or anonymised insights are shared, and data exchange occurs within a governed and interoperable ecosystem. This enables cross-organisational collaboration without compromising individual privacy, and supports new economic models in which organisations access high-quality insights while users retain control over their raw data.

Initiatives such as Gaia-X contribute to this vision by providing a federated infrastructure that ensures interoperability, trust, and regulatory compliance at the ecosystem level. Integration with the Eclipse Dataspace Protocol and CEN/CENELEC trusted data transaction standards would be natural next steps, ensuring long-term compatibility with the evolving European digital infrastructure.

### Stakeholder Considerations

The proposed architecture offers distinct benefits across stakeholder groups. **Media companies** stand to benefit from reduced data liability and access to richer, user-consented insights, gaining a more accurate picture of audience behaviour without the legal and reputational risks of opaque data collection. **Users** gain transparency, control, and portability over their data, though it must be acknowledged that managing fine-grained permission settings may introduce a degree of complexity that not all users will find straightforward. **Policymakers** benefit from an approach that is structurally aligned with European data protection strategy and initiatives such as Gaia-X, offering a practical model for how regulatory ambitions can be translated into working systems.



### Limitations and Challenges

Several challenges remain that must be honestly acknowledged. **Adoption** is perhaps the most significant: realising the benefits of this architecture requires industry-wide cooperation and meaningful incentives, without which the network effects that currently favour centralised platforms will continue to dominate. **Performance** is also a concern, as distributed systems inherently introduce latency compared to centralised architectures, which could affect the responsiveness of personalisation features that users have come to expect. At the user level, the risk of **usability** fatigue is real: if managing consent settings becomes too burdensome, users may disengage from the governance process entirely, undermining the sovereignty goals the system is designed to serve. Finally, **standardisation** across heterogeneous organisations requires ongoing governance, a coordination challenge that technical standards alone cannot fully resolve.

These limitations do not invalidate the proposed approach, but they define the conditions under which it can succeed. Addressing them is as much a sociotechnical challenge as a technical one.

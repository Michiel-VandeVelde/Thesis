## Abstract
{:#abstract}

Streaming platforms generate vast amounts of behavioural data, yet users retain little control over how this data is collected, stored, or shared. Current architectures centralise data within individual platforms, fragmenting the user's digital footprint across services while limiting transparency and sovereignty.

This bachelor's thesis investigates which key design decisions must be made when building media behaviour profiles across multiple media services in a user-centric way.
The proposed architecture is grounded in the Solid ecosystem and TrustFlows framework, combining decentralised personal data storage with policy-driven access control. Design decisions are guided by five core principles: European data sovereignty, open-source implementation, adherence to open standards (including W3C RDF, SHACL, ODRL, and LDES; the Eclipse Dataspace Protocol; and CEN/CENELEC trusted data transactions), interoperability across autonomous stakeholders, and the formulation of competency questions that define what the system must be capable of answering.

The research is validated through stakeholder consultation with media organisations including VRT and MediaNet Vlaanderen, and culminates in a conceptual architecture, a proof-of-concept implementation, and a structured set of design decisions for cross-service media behaviour profiling. The results demonstrate that a user-centric, interoperable, and legally compliant media data architecture is both theoretically sound and practically achievable within the European digital landscape.
